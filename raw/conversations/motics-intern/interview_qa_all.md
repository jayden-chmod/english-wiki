### Q13. Walk me through CogEn.

> "CogEn is a conversational system built around belief change modeling. Most AI systems treat knowledge as static. I wanted to build something that reflects reality — how beliefs change over time."
>
> "When the user says something, the system transforms the input into a structured format. It retrieves relevant information from the knowledge graph using vector embedding search and node traversal. The system generates a response from that."
>
> "After responding, the system asynchronously updates the knowledge graph. Existing beliefs are reinforced, weakened, or terminated based on the new input."
>
> "This process applies directly to the Scribe Agent. It already extracts symptoms and duration from conversations. If those are structured into a knowledge graph, ICD suggestion becomes a graph search problem. And if you combine that with patient history and medical knowledge, the system can make more specific decisions. The graph can also enforce hard clinical rules. What must be present. What cannot. Those are cases a probabilistic model cannot reliably catch."
>
> "I read that your Scribe Agent has already reached 90% accuracy in ICD coding. The remaining 10% is where a knowledge graph can help."

### Q22. AI를 실제 production 환경에 적용했을 때 가장 어려웠던 점은?

> "The hardest part was consistency. LLM-based systems produce variable output. Controlling that in production took significant effort."
>
> "Three things mattered most. Prompt design, context management, and structured output."
>
> "Prompts need to be calibrated. Too specific and the model gets trapped in the instruction. Too general and you lose control. Finding the right balance requires iteration. There is no shortcut. That said, if your test suite is thorough and concrete enough, an agent loop can handle much of that iteration for you."
>
> "Context management is more fundamental. When you give the model too much information at once, performance drops and hallucination increases. The solution is decomposition. Decisions that can be made independently should be made independently. You inject only what is relevant at each step. By the time the final decision is made, the model is working with refined, focused information."
>
> "Structured output is the third piece. Rather than using the model's raw output directly, you define a template in advance. The model's job is to fill in the structured pieces, validated against a schema, and the server assembles the final response by injecting those pieces into the template. The model never owns the shape of the output. Your system does."
>
> "Pitwall is built exactly this way. The pipeline runs in four to five steps. Context that is not needed at a given step is stored separately and injected only when required. Every output is schema-checked before the next step runs."
>
> "That is what made it reliable enough for production."

### Q24. How would you architect a new feature end-to-end?

> "First, I clarify the goal. What the requester asks for and what actually needs to happen are often different. I share what I see from my side and we align."
>
> "After that, I think about what is needed to achieve that goal, whether it is feasible, and what side effects it could cause."
>
> "I have an example where clarifying the goal mattered. At SSG.COM, expired products were coming out of AutoStore. The request was to stop that from happening."
>
> "I analysed the code and found the bin selection was picking most-stock first. I asked colleagues why. It used to be expiry-first. But that slowed everything down. So they switched to most-stock for throughput. That meant the real goal was achieving both. Simply reverting to expiry-first would break throughput."
>
> "I sorted bins by expiry first and gave the bins needed to cover daily consumption equal priority. I also added a mode switch for high-volume periods. I shared this with the stakeholders. They agreed and asked if the switch could be automated. I added that as an additional layer."
>
> "That's the process. Clarify the goal. Check feasibility and side effects. Design. Share. Build on the feedback."

### Q41. What challenges did you face when working with React, and how did you solve them?

> "At SSG.COM, the Touch Grid was a core UI component used for a wide variety of warehouse tasks. Each task type required a different layout, so requests for new designs came in frequently."
>
> "Rather than building one-off designs each time, I defined a set of standard types that teams could configure. I also allowed the layout definition to accept Component, logic, and event handlers directly. That gave teams full control without requiring a new component for every variation."
>
> "The second challenge was re-rendering. When state changes trigger a full re-render, scroll position resets and in-progress data is lost."
>
> "I designed the hook and state update logic to minimise unnecessary re-renders. For state that had to persist across renders, I used Context API to avoid unnecessary re-render. All states stayed stable."

### Q18. Can you go deeper on the Vehicle Resource Optimisation system?

> "This project predicts order volumes and calculates how many vehicles each centre needs. A manager reviews the result, and then the system automatically syncs it to the legacy system."
>
> "Per-vehicle throughput increased by 10.5%. And teams used to plan fleet resources manually every week. Now they set a monthly plan and the system updates daily. That cut the workload significantly."
>
> "There are several parts to it. Order forecasting, vehicle optimisation, legacy integration. If any of those interest you, I can go deeper on that specific area."

**Follow-up: Order forecasting (if probed):**

> "One thing worth mentioning is that we chose a rule-based approach over machine learning. The reason is that the prediction targets keep changing. Each centre and store doesn't have a fixed postcode assignment. It changes depending on the situation. Store holidays also shift."
>
> "So when those changes happen, the patterns that the ML model learned stop working. Fixing that would require significant data engineering effort. So we went with rule-based."
>
> "The logic itself uses two years of historical data and the previous month's data. We calculate ratios by month, by week, and by day of week from the two-year history. Then we apply those ratios to the previous month's actual numbers to predict the next month."

**Follow-up: Vehicle optimisation (if probed):**

> "The key challenge was that every centre had different constraints. To handle that, I built a criteria management interface so each centre could define and control its own rules."
>
> "Based on those rules, the optimisation runs as a greedy algorithm. It starts from the default vehicle count and adds or removes one vehicle at a time until it hits the target order rate."
>
> "There was also a demand transfer problem. If a centre changes its dispatch schedule, say from four times a day to three, the remaining demand has to go somewhere. I built logic to handle how that demand shifts across the remaining dispatch slots."

**Follow-up: Legacy integration (if probed):**

> "The optimisation produces a monthly vehicle plan. The delivery manager discusses it with the vendors and confirms the final numbers. Once confirmed, I generate an operational plan from that."
>
> "The legacy system manages dispatch on a day-of-week basis. Before this, the manager had to manually switch to next week's plan every time a day closed. Every day. I built a process that takes the monthly plan and automatically applies the right daily configuration. That removed the daily manual work entirely."

### Q21. Walk me through the multi-objective optimisation.

> "Expired products were coming out of AutoStore. AutoStore stores items in bins, which are basically boxes stacked in a grid. The weird thing is, these were popular items, not slow movers. When I looked at the bin selection logic, it was picking bins with the most stock. Newly replenished bins always have the highest count. So those were always picked first and older stock just sat there until it expired."
>
> "I asked colleagues why it was set up that way. It used to be expiry-first. But that meant waiting on one bin until it's empty, which slowed everything down. So they switched to most-stock to speed things up."
>
> "What I did was sort all bins by expiry first. Then I calculated expected daily consumption and gave the bins needed to cover that equal priority. Within a single day, the order might not be strictly expiry-first. But by the end of the day, older stock gets consumed first."
>
> "I also added a mode switch for high-volume periods. When tasks per minute, remaining workload, and remaining time until deadline hit a certain threshold, the system automatically switches to throughput-first. So both objectives are covered."

## Untiered

### Q. Tell me about a challenge you faced and how you solved it.

> "At SSG.com, I faced many challenges. But I always used the same approach to solve them."
>
> "First principle is, do not believe what's in front of me. Instead, I try to find the real goal behind it first."
>
> "To do that, I ask myself: What is the root cause? What do I need to do to fix it? Are there any constraints and side effects? If new sub-topics come up, I just repeat the same loop."
>
> "One day, an expired item came out of our AutoStore during picking. The request was simple. Please make sure expired items don't come out anymore."
>
> "The easy fix would have been to check for a bug in the expiration validation. But I thought I should first check the expired item itself."
>
> "Surprisingly, it was a fast-moving product. Frequently ordered and restocked. That felt strange to me, so I looked deeper into the bin selection logic."
>
> "I found that bins with more stock were getting higher priority. I thought there must be a history behind this, so I asked my team lead. He told me that a third party vendor had tried expiration-first logic before, but it caused serious picking delays. So the team just switched to stock quantity priority instead."
>
> "That meant simply changing to expiration-first would likely cause the same problem again. The bin selection logic itself needed to be improved."
>
> "So I came up with a different solution. We already know how many orders are coming in each day. So I sorted bins by expiration date, and gave equal priority to all bins needed for that day's volume."
>
> "When I talked to the business team, I realized their real goal was not just preventing expired items from coming out. It was making sure items close to expiring get consumed before they expire. That was the real objective."
>
> "This is how I handle challenges. Find the root cause, understand what people really need, and think about side effects before solving."
